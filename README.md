# proDAD: A History of Innovation in Video Effects & Optimization

Welcome to the comprehensive history and reference guide of **proDAD GmbH**, a globally recognized leader in developing high-quality video editing tools, visual effects, and image optimization software. Since 1990, proDAD has empowered digital artists and video enthusiasts with solutions that enhance video quality and streamline creative workflows.

### The Founders & The Architect
The company was founded by **Andreas Huber** and **Holger Burkarth**, whose shared vision established the foundation for decades of success in the digital imaging industry.

A unique and defining characteristic of proDAD is **Holger Burkarth's** role. As the company's technological mastermind, Burkarth has served as the sole software developer behind the entire product portfolio from the beginning until now. He has personally engineered every piece of software, managing the entire development life-cycle from initial conceptualization to final, market-ready product, ensuring a singular, cohesive vision across all proDAD solutions.

### Evolution of Platforms
The proDAD journey began in **1990** with deep roots in the **Commodore Amiga** scene. As digital video technology evolved, the company successfully transitioned its expertise to MacroSystem's legendary **Casablanca** set-top editing systems.

After the era of dedicated hardware, proDAD shifted its focus to the **Windows PC** market and set the standard for video stabilization and effects plug-ins. Demonstrating remarkable adaptability and longevity, the software suite was expanded and ported to **Mac OS X** and **Linux** over time, ensuring that creators across all major operating systems have access to proDAD’s powerful tools.



### Origins and the Spark (Pre-1990)

The story of proDAD began years before its official incorporation, fueled by a fascination with emerging technology. 

In the winter of **1983**, while completing his apprenticeship as a mechanic, Holger Burkarth attended vocational school once a week. During lunch breaks, he and his classmates often visited the *Karstadt* department store. On one such visit, he stopped abruptly in front of a small display featuring a television set. The screen showed nothing but a blue background with cryptic text and numbers. Fascinated, they watched until another visitor began typing on the keyboard, causing the screen to flash in a kaleidoscope of colors. This was a key moment for Holger: seeing a computer in action—specifically, a **Commodore 64**—for the first time.

The concept of a computer was not entirely new to him. He had watched scenes involving them in movies with rapt attention and had already engaged with the subject theoretically. At the age of 13, for instance, he had planned to equip his bicycle with an automatic gear shifting system. While the mechanics seemed solvable to him, he wanted the control logic to shift gears up and down autonomously based on rotation frequency. Having read in the educational series *"Wie geht das?"* (How does it work?) that computers were built from logical elements, he began sketching his own computer design on several A3 sheets of his school pad. Although he knew he could never actually build it, the exercise was formative—his interest in computing was permanently ignited.

Initially, a large portion of his apprentice wages went toward train tickets, but by the winter of **1984**, he had saved enough to purchase his own C64. Four months prior, he had already scoured the library for a BASIC programming book. The selection was sparse: he found only a small paperback with a dark blue border and fewer than fifty pages. Nevertheless, it was a start.

When purchasing the C64, the salesperson attempted to sell him a floppy disk drive, but it cost nearly as much as the computer itself. Unable to afford it, Holger opted for a **Datasette** (tape drive)—a decision that would later prove cumbersome. At home, the C64 was connected to the family television in the living room. Holger would sit directly on the floor to program. Every evening, the setup had to be dismantled so his parents could watch the news and movies undisturbed. It wasn't until he bought a small black-and-white television that he could establish a permanent workspace with his own desk.

As his programming experience grew, so did the complexity of his programs—and the problems. Saving and loading via the Datasette was tedious and time-consuming. If a program wasn't saved in time, a crash or power-off meant it was irretrievably lost. In his third year of apprenticeship, with slightly higher wages, he finally fulfilled his wish for a **floppy disk drive**.

Through publications like the *"Commodore C64 Magazin,"* he absorbed technical details and programming techniques. Initially, he worked in **BASIC**, the standard language of the C64. However, an article highlighting the immense speed of machine language compared to BASIC sparked his ambition. He began compiling binary codes from tables manually and calculating jump distances for conditional commands by hand. Since commercial Assembler software was expensive, he simply wrote his own Assembler in BASIC. Soon realizing that BASIC was too slow for larger projects, he used his first Assembler to develop a second one—this time written entirely in machine code—which operated significantly faster.

There were few C64 owners in his circle; Alex and Andreas were the only ones he knew. They met to play games and exchange knowledge. while Holger was deeply engrossed in programming, his friends were more interested in gaming and graphics. Together, they dreamed of developing their own game: Holger would handle the coding, and the others would do the design. Using his new Assembler, he developed a scrollable playfield where objects could be positioned and moved—a promising foundation. However, his collaborators' enthusiasm quickly waned, and the project fizzled out.

Another project involved partially recreating his pocket calculator on the C64. This calculator had played a special role in his youth. As a twelve-year-old, bored and flipping through the *"Quelle"* mail-order catalog, he discovered a solar-powered model that required no batteries. His parents couldn't afford it immediately but promised it for Christmas. With this device, he encountered buttons and functions that were completely new to him—specifically trigonometric functions. The manual not only explained how to use them but also dedicated a section to the mathematical theory behind them, complete with examples. Reading this awakened a lasting interest in mathematics—topics that had not yet been covered in school.

Beyond software, hardware increasingly appealed to him. After reading a magazine article about a **light pen**—a device that could detect positions on the screen, as the mouse was not yet common—he was captivated by the idea of drawing and writing directly on the monitor. Following the printed schematic, he built the device himself, placing a photodiode into the tip of an empty ballpoint pen casing. The combination of hardware and software opened up a new world of experimentation.

However, not all hardware experiments were successful. After seeing a plotter on a TV show that could draw on paper, Holger attempted to build one. A wiring error during the construction resulted in severe damage to his C64. The repair cost almost as much as a new unit. Following this incident, he decided to concentrate more heavily on software.

He developed analysis and monitor tools to examine and optimize other programs. This work led to significant optimizations of the C64's internal software. Most notably, he dramatically accelerated the data transfer rate between the C64 and the floppy drive. Another challenge he tackled was the rapid rendering of geometric shapes—something taken for granted today but requiring great skill at the time. The desire to move wireframe models in three-dimensional space in real-time occupied him for a long period, and he also gained early experience by writing his own sound editor.

What began with a blue screen in a department store evolved into an intensive mastery of both hardware and software for Holger Burkarth, laying the technical foundation for all his future developments.


When Commodore introduced the successor **Amiga** in 1986, Holger Burkarth switched to this advanced technology in **1987**. One of his significant development projects during this time was re-programming a version of the famous *DPaint* software, a process through which he mastered the intricacies of computer pixel graphics. Furthermore, he wrote real-time 2D and 3D graphics programs, allowing him to intimately understand the Amiga's hardware architecture. This deep technical dive provided the essential toolkit and expertise required for his future achievements. Over these years, he remained entirely self-taught, mastering various programming languages and developing a fundamental understanding of computer technology.

### The Breakthrough with Fokus-Film

The pivotal moment arrived in **1989** when the production company **Fokus-Film** settled in the vicinity, leading to a professional connection. Burkarth accepted a freelance commission to create a spectacular video transition effect—a task he undertook in his spare time.

At that time, computer animations on home systems were notorious for stuttering and lacking fluidity. However, Burkarth envisioned a method to render movement much more smoothly, making it appear significantly more natural. Within just **one week**, he successfully implemented this concept on the Amiga hardware. The result was breathtaking; he had achieved a level of quality and smoothness previously unseen on a home computer. This technical triumph was the direct catalyst for the decision to found proDAD.

### A Strategic Partnership

To turn this innovation into a company, Holger Burkarth teamed up with his long-time friend, **Andreas Huber**. They established a clear division of labor that would define the company's structure: the responsibilities were shared across two sets of shoulders. Andreas Huber took charge of all business management and operational tasks, creating an environment where Holger Burkarth could dedicate himself **exclusively to software development**, free from the distractions of administrative duties and management.


### The creation of the iconic logo design

When the company was officially established in early 1990, the name was quickly decided upon. During this period, Holger Burkarth was experimenting with a variety of technologies, particularly programming a pen plotter, which draws lines and circles using commands.

He used this technical environment as the basis for the company’s visual identity, choosing to 'program' the logo via code rather than drawing it manually. He imposed a strict design constraint on himself: the outline of the logo had to consist of a single, unbroken line that never intersected itself. This mathematical approach resulted in the distinctive proDAD lettering. The two wings and shadow, which are seen today, were added later to complete the design.



### The Birth of Adorage and Trade Fair Success (1990–1991)

With the core concept proven, work began on a dedicated application. The goal was to develop software capable of calculating video effects and playing them back using the new smooth animation technique, empowering users to overlay transition effects onto their videos in real-time. Holger Burkarth developed this ambitious project entirely in his spare time while still maintaining his regular day job.

In late 1990, the founders traveled to the **Amiga trade fair in Cologne**, hoping to find potential interest in their prototype. The response was immediate: during their very first presentation, direct contact was established with Commodore right on the show floor. In subsequent meetings, connections were forged with established distribution companies, and it was these industry professionals who suggested the name for the debut product: **"Adorage."**

It took nearly another year to finalize the software. At the **1991 Amiga trade fair**, proDAD officially presented Adorage at their distributor's booth. The distributor had produced crates of product boxes in anticipation, yet the entire stock sold out in less than two days. For the remainder of the event, the company could only accept pre-orders. Massive crowds gathered around Holger Burkarth's station, where he tirelessly demonstrated Adorage and answered questions. Meanwhile, his co-founder Andreas Huber was busy forging crucial new contacts, laying the strategic foundation that would allow proDAD to separate from the distributor and operate independently by the following year.
